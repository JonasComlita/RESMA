RESMA is not primarily a tool company like Excel. It is not primarily an extension company like Honey. And it should be very careful about becoming “an advertiser platform like YouTube.” What it actually is, if it works, is an independent recommendation intelligence company.

That means the closest analogs are:

Ground News: comparative view of what different groups are being shown
SEMrush / Ahrefs: intelligence layer over an opaque platform
Nielsen / Comscore / Sportradar: third-party measurement and predictive signal
Kayak / Hopper: value comes from watching continuously over time, not from one query
That’s the whole game. You are not selling compute. You are selling:

observatory coverage
historical depth
cross-cohort comparison
early signal about how recommendation systems are moving
So I would make three hard calls.

1. Do not lead with per-call pricing
Per-call API pricing is the wrong default for this business.

It makes sense only for truly expensive marginal-cost features, mainly:

LLM-generated interpretations
white-glove report generation if it becomes materially costly
maybe bulk export jobs later
For the core product, flat subscriptions fit better:

people are paying for access to a living observatory
not for each forecast or map click
metering the core analytics will feel arbitrary and cheap
2. Do not describe this as an advertiser platform
You were right to call that out.

The right framing is:

independent pre-impression intelligence
organic recommendation intelligence
third-party observatory of what platforms are actually surfacing
That matters a lot. “Advertiser platform” makes you sound like optimization middleware for targeting people harder. That is both strategically weaker and ethically riskier.

The stronger version is:

brands, agencies, creators, and researchers use RESMA to understand how recommendation systems behave
RESMA does not sell impressions
RESMA does not sell personal targeting
RESMA sells aggregate, independent intelligence about recommendation behavior
That’s a premium product if it’s real.

3. Contributors should remain free
This part of the conversation was dead on.

Contributors are the supply side. They should get:

personal transparency
bubble/discovery tools
research participation
maybe lightweight creator-facing self-insight if they also create
But they should not be the main revenue source. Charging them heavily is backwards.

The money should come from:

individual creators: flat monthly subscription
agencies / brands: premium subscription or annual contract
research institutions / media orgs / NGOs: licensed aggregate access
API / MCP consumers: subscription access with tiered limits, not nickel-and-dime per call
So if I were setting the business model today, it would look like this:

Free Contributor

extension
personal observatory
bubble score / discovery basics
participation in the dataset
Creator Pro

flat monthly
audience forecast
recommendation maps
aggregate briefs
historical trend access
Agency / Brand Intelligence

monthly or annual, probably annual once validated
multi-client workspaces
saved reports
white-labeled exports
cohort monitoring
competitor reach snapshots
recommendation gap reports
Institutional / Research License

aggregate-only dataset access
historical trend slices
export rights
maybe restricted API/MCP access
Usage-based overages only where cost is real

AI narrative generation
heavy exports
enterprise bulk jobs
My honest caution: the codebase is already drifting toward an agency-first surface. You can see it in the package tiers, report runs, and report shares. That is not necessarily wrong. But if the company story becomes “we help advertisers target better,” you will lose the moral high ground and probably the most defensible brand.

The better story is:

We measure the algorithm independently
We help people understand what it is doing
We help creators, agencies, researchers, and institutions act on that intelligence without exposing contributor-level data
That’s strong. That can become a real company.

If I were CEO, the near-term go-to-market would be:

Keep contributor acquisition free and privacy-first.
Sell Creator Pro as the first paid self-serve product.
Sell Agency Pilot as white-glove, high-touch, not mass self-serve yet.
Treat MCP/API access as a distribution and integration channel, not the core business model.
Keep the product language centered on independent recommendation intelligence, not ad targeting.
My blunt verdict:
The best version of RESMA is “Bloomberg terminal / Ground News / SEMrush for recommendation systems,” not “adtech API for social platforms.”
Stay there. That’s where the moat and the integrity are.
